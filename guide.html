1. What is Enduro Manager?

Enduro Manager is a browser-based tool for planning and running endurance sim-racing events (like iRacing enduros). It helps a team work out fuel strategy, tyre wear, pit windows, and driver rotations before the race — then track it live, stint by stint, once the green flag drops.

The app is organised around one race at a time, with three tabs inside each race:

  • Crew — race info, live/local time, your driver roster, and the visual race schedule.
  • Strategy — the calculator: enter race duration, fuel and tyre numbers to generate a full stint-by-stint plan. You can build up to three versions (A/B/C) side by side.
  • Race — the live execution view used during the event, with a large numeric keypad for logging each pit stop as it happens.

There's no installation — it runs entirely in a web browser, and every race you create is saved automatically as you type. This guide walks through every control on all three tabs, including a few behaviours that aren't obvious the first time you see them.

2. Signing in and creating a race

2.1 Signing in

When you first open the app you'll see the sign-in screen. Sign in with Google to create and manage your races — this is required, since every race is saved to your account and synced automatically.

Sign-in screen — sign in with Google to create and manage your races.

2.2 The home screen

After signing in you land on the home screen, which lists every race you've created as a card. Click a card to reopen a race, or start fresh with "+ New Race" in the top right.

Left: an empty home screen. Right: once created, a race appears as a card — click it to reopen, or the × to delete it.

Quick start
  • Click "+ New Race" — the app opens straight into the new race, on the Crew tab.
  • Use "← Races" (top left, inside a race) at any time to return to your race list — your work is saved automatically, no save button needed.

3. Building your strategy

Start on the Strategy tab — this is where you turn race length, fuel, and tyre numbers into a full stint plan. Everything recalculates live as you type, so it's safe to experiment before committing on the Race tab.

The Strategy tab: four input panels along the top, a live summary row, then the stint table and gantt chart below.

3.1 The four input panels

Race duration

Set the total race time and your average lap time. These two numbers drive every other calculation — total laps, stint count, and pit windows all derive from them.

Fuel

  • Fuel consumption — litres used per lap.
  • Tank capacity and Starting fuel — starting fuel can be less than a full tank; it defaults to tank capacity.
  • Fuel margin — a safety buffer, in laps, subtracted from every stint's maximum length.

Tyre wear calculator

Toggle On to have the planner project tyre wear forward across the race and flag stints where tread will drop below your warning threshold.

  • Compound badges — Dry, Soft, Medium, Hard, Wet, Inter. Click one to mark which compound the tread readings below belong to; the selected badge fills with that compound's colour while the rest stay outlined and grey. Purely a label — it doesn't change the wear calculation, just makes it easier to tell compounds apart at a glance if you're comparing them across strategies.
  • Laps driven — how many laps are already on the current set of tyres at the moment you take the tread readings below. This is not the race length; it's how old the tyres were when you measured them.
  • Warning below — the tread percentage that counts as worn out. Anywhere in the app that shows a tread number — the Strategy table, the gantt chart, and the tread donuts on the Race tab — turns that number green at or above this value and orange below it, so it's a consistent visual cutoff, not just a strategy-tab setting.

Tread readings

These four boxes (FL, FR, RL, RR) are outer / mid / inner tread-depth percentages for each tyre, and they are current measured readings, not fresh-tyre numbers — i.e. the state of the tyres after "Laps driven" laps have already been run on them, not 100% new-tyre values.

The app doesn't measure tyres itself — you get these numbers from telemetry or a third-party tool (e.g. Garage 61 or your sim's own tyre wear readout) after a run, and type them in here yourself.

The calculator averages each tyre's three readings, then averages all four tyres into one figure, and divides how much tread has been lost (100% minus that average) by the laps driven to get a wear-per-lap rate. That rate is what's then projected forward stint by stint to predict when tread will cross the warning line and a tyre change should be scheduled.

3.2 Reading the output

  • Race summary row — total laps, stints, pit stops, max laps/stint, tyre sets required, drivers needed.
  • Stint strategy table — lap ranges, fuel load, earliest/latest pit lap, a manual pit override, assigned driver, remaining tread, and tyre-change flag.
  • Stint strategy overview — a colour-coded gantt chart of the whole race, with pit-window markers and tyre-change dots.

3.3 Overriding a pit lap manually

Every stint row has a Manual pit field, shown as "auto" by default. Type a lap number to force that stint to end on an exact lap — useful for a mandatory pit window or a full-course caution.

3.4 Copying a strategy

Above the race duration panel, a small Copy control lets you duplicate one strategy slot's entire input set into another — every field on this tab, not just the ones you've changed. Pick a source strategy on the left, a destination on the right, and click Copy.

Caution
  • Copy fully overwrites the destination strategy — there's no merge and no undo. If Strategy B already has its own inputs, copying into it discards them.
  • If the destination is the strategy currently on screen, the tab refreshes immediately to show the copied values; if it's not on screen, the copy still happens in the background and appears next time you switch to it.

3.5 Comparing strategy time cost

The Time cost settings panel, just above the stint table, estimates how much slower or faster each strategy (A, B, C) actually is in total race time — not just fuel or laps, but a modeled time cost per stint based on tyre wear, fuel weight, and pit stop duration. Each strategy keeps its own copy of every setting below, so a "conservative" plan and an "aggressive" plan can genuinely use different tyre and fuel assumptions rather than sharing one global set of numbers.

  • Fuel benefit — seconds gained per litre the car is running lighter than a full tank; this is what rewards a strategy that burns fuel down before its next stop instead of topping up early.
  • Tyre wear curve — instead of a single flat number, tyre cost is now a curve: click "Edit curve" to open an editor with five draggable points from fresh (100% tread) to fully worn (0%). The default shape stays nearly flat for the first half of a tyre's life, then rises sharply near the end — matching how real tyre degradation behaves (see sourcing below).
  • Tyre change / Refuel rate / In-out lap — the stationary time modeled for a pit stop: whichever of tyre-change time or refuel time is longer (they happen in parallel), plus a flat penalty for the in-lap and out-lap.
  • Fuel saving — a slower, fuel-efficient lap time and its reduced fuel-per-lap rate. Toggle the green/grey slider in the stint table's "Pace mode" column to flag a stint as fuel-saving; its length and fuel use recalculate using these numbers instead of the normal pace.

Fill these in to match your car and pit crew, then click "Compare time cost." The panel needs at least one strategy with a completed stint plan to compare — build out Strategy A and B (and C, if you're using it) first.

The comparison works by simulating each strategy lap-by-lap for the full race duration and counting how many laps it actually completes — not by adding up a single "total time" figure. The Fastest strategy is whichever one completes the most laps; if two strategies complete the same number of laps, the tiebreaker is which one reaches that lap count sooner. Every other strategy shows its gap as either a lap deficit ("−2 laps") or a time gap at an equal lap count, whichever applies.

Click the small arrow on any stint row in the results to expand a per-stint breakdown — pace cost, tyre cost, pit cost, and fuel benefit for that specific stint, so you can see exactly which part of a strategy is costing or saving time rather than just the stint's total.

Where these defaults come from

The starting values aren't arbitrary — they're built from commonly-cited motorsport engineering figures, then adapted for a GT3 endurance context:

  • Fuel weight effect — published rule-of-thumb figures from F1 and sports car strategy analysis put the lap time cost of extra fuel weight in roughly the 0.01–0.045 seconds per kilogram range depending on car and track (see discussion threads on Autosport's Technical Forum, and typical figures quoted by sim-racing fuel calculators for GT3-class cars). Converted from kilograms to litres using petrol's density (≈0.75 kg/L) and scaled down for a heavier, higher-drag GT3 car, this lands the default fuel benefit at a modest per-litre figure rather than the sharper numbers seen in open-wheel racing.
  • Tyre wear curve shape — sim-racing fuel and stint calculators commonly cite lap times increasing by roughly 0.5–2 seconds per lap as a tyre stint wears on, but that number is not constant across the stint: degradation is widely described as staying fairly flat for the first half of a tyre's life before rising sharply near the end. The default five-point curve reflects that shape — small, near-flat values from 100% down to 50% tread, then a steep climb from 25% to 0% — rather than the single flat number the app used before the curve editor existed.

Treat these as a sensible starting point, not gospel — every number and every point on the curve is editable per strategy, so replace them with your own car's real data as soon as you have it (pit crew timing sheets, or lap time deltas pulled from telemetry across a stint).

The default tyre wear curve shape — flat through the first half of tyre life, then a sharp rise near the end.

Tip
  • If you edit any input after calculating, the results panel dims and a "recalculate" tag appears — the numbers on screen are now stale until you press Compare time cost again.
  • Switching between the A / B / C sub-tabs does not mark results stale — only an actual edit to the currently-viewed strategy does.
Tip
  • Build up to three strategies (A / B / C) via the sub-tabs — good for comparing a conservative plan against an aggressive one.
  • "Auto distribute drivers" (also on the Crew tab) spreads drivers evenly across stints instead of assigning each by hand.
  • Values save automatically as you type — no save button on this tab.

4. Setting up your crew

The Crew tab is your race's control room: race identity, live clocks for coordinating an international team, your driver roster, and the full visual schedule.

The Crew tab, with a race name, timezone, and start time set.

4.1 Race info and timing

  • Give the race a track name at the top left — this also appears on the race card on your home screen.
  • Right below Track, pick a Car from the dropdown (every current iRacing GT3) or choose "Custom…" to type any other car — this also appears on the race card on your home screen, above the date.
  • Set race start date, time, and timezone on the right — the app calculates the exact race end time in both local and UTC.
Important — set the timezone first
  • The timezone dropdown starts blank on a new race — it does not default to your browser's timezone.
  • Race start time, the countdown, and the Race roster panel all silently stay empty until a timezone is explicitly chosen.
  • If the roster still says "Set race start time and strategy inputs to see roster" after entering a date/time, check that a timezone is actually selected.

4.2 Adding your crew

Add team members in the Crew list — each card can be a Driver, Spotter, Engineer, or custom role. Click "Details" to expand a card and add an iRating, email, or notes; drag by the handle to reorder.

  • "Auto Distribute Drivers" spreads drivers evenly across stints and shows a "Distributed!" confirmation.
  • The driver utilisation donuts update immediately, showing each driver's share of total laps.
  • "Update" re-syncs the crew list and roster after changing driver assignments elsewhere.

4.3 The race roster

Once a timezone is set and drivers are assigned, the roster renders the schedule as a timeline — one column per crew member, coloured blocks for each stint, plotted against local and UTC time down the side.

Click a stint block to open its detail modal — add a free-text note (e.g. "push hard", "save tyres") and see driver, time window, duration, laps, and fuel at a glance.

The timeline doesn't start at the green flag — it starts 30 minutes before it. That lead-in is deliberate space for everything that happens before racing actually begins: driver briefing, grid formation, final checks. Stint 1 always begins 30 minutes down from the top of the grid, never at the very top.

The 30-minute buffer sits between the time you set and the first stint — custom slots can go anywhere in it.

To schedule something in that window — or anywhere else on the timeline — either click directly on a member's column to drop a new slot at that time, or drag a member's card from the crew list onto the schedule. Both open the same "Add slot" modal: give it a label (e.g. "Driver briefing", "Fuelling", "On comms"), an optional longer note, a colour, and a start/end time. An existing slot can be dragged to move it, dragged from its top or bottom edge to resize it, or clicked to edit or delete it.

A slot turns red and won't save if it overlaps something else already on that member's timeline — either another custom slot or one of their driving stints. Each member's own timezone (if set on their crew card) shows alongside the race's local time inside both stint blocks and custom slots, so a crew spread across time zones can each read their own schedule correctly.

5. Running the race live

When it's time to race, switch to the Race tab — the operational view your crew uses to log what actually happens and compare it against the plan.

5.1 Sync from Strategy

The Race tab starts empty. Click "↓ Sync from Strategy" to pull in whichever strategy (A, B, or C) you want to execute, chosen via the A/B/C selector. This locks in your plan as the race baseline.

The Race tab after syncing — the active stint is highlighted, with tread donuts and the numeric keypads ready for pit-lane input.

5.2 The stint card and numeric keypad

The active stint expands into two large numeric keypads built for quick pit-lane input: one for actual pit lap, one for fuel added. Before entry, fields show the planner's pre-filled estimate, dimmed.

Tap the keypad digits to enter real values as they happen — e.g. 2 then 7 sets pit lap to 27, and 4 then 9 sets fuel-in-car to 49 L. ⌫ deletes the last digit.

  • Actual pit lap — shifts every remaining stint's expected pit lap to stay accurate.
  • Fuel in car — the next stint's fuel-to-add recalculates from reality; the app also derives an actual fuel-per-lap figure shown at the top of the tab.
  • Tread donuts — projected tyre wear at stint start and end, calculated from your Strategy tab tyre settings; each donut turns orange once it's projected to drop below the warning threshold you set there.
  • Tyre change toggle — click to mark or unmark a tyre change at the pit into this stint, overriding the automatic calculation.

With both fields filled, tap the checkered-flag GO panel to lock in the stint. It marks Done, and the next stint becomes "NOW" — recalculated using your actual fuel and lap data.

Note
  • GO stays disabled until both the actual pit lap and fuel-in-car fields have a value.
  • On the final stint there's no real pit stop, but a lap number (typically the last lap) is still needed before GO marks the race complete.
  • An "← Undo" link on the active card reverses the most recent GO.

5.3 Keeping it two-way

Click "↑ Push to Strategy" to send live adjustments back to the Strategy tab. Sync and Push are separate one-way actions — nothing pushes automatically.

Caution
  • "↺ Reset Race" clears every logged pit lap, fuel entry, and tyre override, and returns to stint 1 — immediately, with no confirmation prompt.

5.4 Desktop, Mobile, and the pop-out overlay

Three controls at the top-right are easy to miss. The Desktop / Mobile toggle does not change the Race tab's own layout — it only sets the size used by the pop-out overlay (640×420 for Desktop, 360×640 for Mobile).

  • ⧉ Pop out overlay — opens the current "NOW" card in a small, always-on-top window (Picture-in-Picture), so a spotter or pit-wall crew can keep it visible over other apps. Needs Chrome or Edge and a synced strategy first.
  • The pop-out has its own live keypad, wired to the same race data — entries there update the main window and vice versa.

5.5 The real mobile view

Separately, the whole Race tab has its own responsive mobile layout that activates automatically on a small screen — useful for running the pit wall from a phone or tablet without the pop-out overlay.

6. Using it on a phone or tablet

Every tab has a purpose-built mobile layout that kicks in automatically below roughly 600px in either dimension — not the desktop layout squeezed smaller, but a different, touch-first set of controls.

The home screen's mobile layout — stacked for a phone screen.

Tip
  • If you resize a desktop window past the mobile threshold and a tab looks stuck on the desktop layout, switch tabs once to force a re-render.
